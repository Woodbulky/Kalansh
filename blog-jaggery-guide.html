Jaggery vs. Sugar: Why Our Grandparents Were Right All Along

By Kalansh Team

Walk into any Indian kitchen built before 1970, and you will find a block of dark jaggery on the shelf. Walk into most kitchens today, and you will find a bag of refined white sugar. This shift happened gradually over the 20th century, driven by the lower cost and longer shelf life of refined sugar. But it came at a nutritional cost that we are only beginning to understand.

What is jaggery, exactly?

Jaggery (gur in Hindi, gul in Marathi) is made by boiling freshly pressed sugarcane juice in open iron pans until it thickens and sets. No chemicals are added. No centrifugation. No bleaching. The natural molasses — the dark, mineral-rich syrup — remains part of the final product.

Refined white sugar, by contrast, strips all of this away. The molasses is removed, the juice is filtered through bone char or activated carbon, and what remains is pure sucrose crystals with zero micronutrients.

The nutritional comparison

Jaggery contains iron, magnesium, potassium, calcium, and small amounts of vitamins B1, B2, B3, B6, and C. Refined sugar contains none of these — it is empty calories, pure and simple.

Jaggery also has a slightly lower glycaemic index than refined sugar, meaning it causes a gentler rise in blood glucose levels. And its natural molasses has been shown in traditional Ayurvedic practice to aid digestion — a reason why a small piece of jaggery has been eaten after meals in Indian households for millennia.

Our organic jaggery

You can taste the difference. Refined sugar tastes sharply sweet and nothing else. Our jaggery tastes sweet and earthy and complex — like it came from the ground, because it did.
