## Blog Post 1

**Title:** Rajgira (Amaranth): The Ancient Superfood Maharashtra Has Always Known
**Slug:** blog-rajgira-benefits.html
**Date:** October 21, 2023
**Author:** Kalansh Team
**Cover image:** `assets/images/blog-rajgira.jpg`
**Excerpt:** Rajgira has been a staple of Maharashtrian fasting food for centuries. But modern nutrition science is just catching up to what our grandmothers already knew.

**Full content:**

Rajgira — known in English as Amaranth — is one of the oldest cultivated crops on earth, with origins tracing back over 8,000 years to Central America and South Asia. In Maharashtra, it has been a beloved fasting food for generations, eaten during Navratri and Ekadashi when grains are avoided.

But rajgira is far more than just a fasting food. Nutritionists now classify it as one of the most complete plant protein sources available — containing all nine essential amino acids, which is rare for a plant-based food.

**What makes Rajgira so special?**

Rajgira is unusually rich in protein compared to other grains, containing roughly 14 grams of protein per 100 grams. It is also one of the best plant-based sources of calcium — making it excellent for growing children, women, and the elderly. Its iron content helps combat anaemia, which remains widespread in rural Maharashtra. And unlike wheat, rajgira is completely gluten-free, making it safe for those with gluten sensitivity.

The combination of rajgira and jaggery — as in our Rajgira Ladoo — is particularly intelligent from a nutritional perspective. Jaggery is rich in iron, and Vitamin C (naturally present in the sugarcane) helps the body absorb that iron more effectively. The two ingredients were paired not by accident but by generations of intuitive food wisdom.

**Why Kalansh uses organic rajgira**

At Kalansh, we source our rajgira from verified organic farmers — rajgira grown without synthetic pesticides or chemical fertilisers. The result is a cleaner, more flavourful seed that pops beautifully and retains its full nutritional profile.

Every Rajgira Ladoo we make is a direct expression of this philosophy: that the best snack food is also good food.

## Blog Post 2

**Title:** Jaggery vs. Sugar: Why Our Grandparents Were Right All Along
**Slug:** blog-jaggery-guide.html
**Date:** November 15, 2023
**Author:** Kalansh Team
**Cover image:** `assets/images/blog-jaggery.jpg`
**Excerpt:** Refined white sugar is a recent invention. Jaggery — gur — has sweetened Indian kitchens for over 3,000 years. Here is what the difference actually means for your body.

**Full content:**

Walk into any Indian kitchen built before 1970, and you will find a block of dark jaggery on the shelf. Walk into most kitchens today, and you will find a bag of refined white sugar. This shift happened gradually over the 20th century, driven by the lower cost and longer shelf life of refined sugar. But it came at a nutritional cost that we are only beginning to understand.

**What is jaggery, exactly?**

Jaggery (gur in Hindi, gul in Marathi) is made by boiling freshly pressed sugarcane juice in open iron pans until it thickens and sets. No chemicals are added. No centrifugation. No bleaching. The natural molasses — the dark, mineral-rich syrup — remains part of the final product.

Refined white sugar, by contrast, strips all of this away. The molasses is removed, the juice is filtered through bone char or activated carbon, and what remains is pure sucrose crystals with zero micronutrients.

**The nutritional comparison**

Jaggery contains iron, magnesium, potassium, calcium, and small amounts of vitamins B1, B2, B3, B6, and C. Refined sugar contains none of these — it is empty calories, pure and simple.

Jaggery also has a slightly lower glycaemic index than refined sugar, meaning it causes a gentler rise in blood glucose levels. And its natural molasses has been shown in traditional Ayurvedic practice to aid digestion — a reason why a small piece of jaggery has been eaten after meals in Indian households for millennia.

**Our organic jaggery**

The jaggery Kalansh uses — and sells as a standalone product — is made from organic sugarcane using a traditional open-pan process in Maharashtra. No chemicals, no additives, no processing aids. Just pure sugarcane juice, heat, and time.

You can taste the difference. Refined sugar tastes sharply sweet and nothing else. Our jaggery tastes sweet and earthy and complex — like it came from the ground, because it did.

## Blog Post 3

**Title:** 90 Women, One Kopargaon Kitchen: The Story Behind Every Kalansh Product
**Slug:** blog-women-empowerment.html
**Date:** December 10, 2023
**Author:** Rohit Kale, Founder
**Cover image:** `assets/images/blog-women.jpg`
**Excerpt:** When Rohit and Vinay Kale started Kalansh in 2019, they had a product idea. But more than that, they had a mission — to create meaningful employment for women in rural Maharashtra who needed it most.

**Full content:**

In 2019, when my brother Vinay and I first set up our small production space in Kopargaon, we started with one craftsman and four women. Today, 90 women work in our 10,000 square-foot facility, and their hands are behind every Rajgira Ladoo, every Chikki, and every block of Organic Jaggery that leaves our doors.

I want to tell you a little about why this matters to me.

**The gap we saw**

Kopargaon, like many small towns in Ahmadnagar district, has a significant number of women who want to work — and are skilled, hardworking, and reliable — but face structural barriers to employment. Many cannot commute to factories in larger cities. Many have children or elderly parents at home. Many were told, implicitly or explicitly, that their place was not in a formal workplace.

When we started producing food products, we made a deliberate choice: we would build our workforce around these women, not despite their constraints, but by designing around them.

**What we built**

The main facility in Kopargaon employs 90 women in production roles — rolling ladoos, preparing chikki, packaging, quality checking. We designed shift timings to allow women to handle school runs and family responsibilities. We set up small production stalls in residential neighbourhoods so women who cannot travel even to our facility can earn from home.

We started a Women's Savings Group — not just wages, but a mechanism for financial planning and long-term stability. We celebrate Raksha Bandhan, Ganesh Utsav, and Makar Sankranti together as a team.

**Why it shows in the product**

There is something different about food made by people who care. You cannot measure it in a lab, but you can taste it. Our women artisans take pride in every ladoo they roll. A misshapen one gets redone. A batch that is slightly off gets flagged. The ISO 22000:2018 certification we earned in 2023 is a formal recognition of the quality standards our team upholds every single day.

When you eat a Kalansh Rajgira Ladoo, you are eating the work of women in Kopargaon who are proud of what they make. We think that is worth knowing.

— Rohit Kale, Founder & CEO, Kalansh
